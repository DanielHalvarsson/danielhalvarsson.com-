The Waking System

How monitoring loops taught my AI agents to exercise judgment

The Unsent Draft

Last month I wrote an important email about a PhD student proposal. I finished it, felt satisfied, and went to pick up my kids from kindergarten and school. The next morning — the day after the deadline — I realized I never hit send.

That was the moment I understood what my AI system needed to become. Not a better tool. A colleague that watches when I can't.

What I Built

I run a personal AI system — six specialized agents managing email, calendar, tasks, notes, relationships, and code. They coordinate through a decentralized handshake protocol: each agent owns its domain and signals when it needs another agent's capabilities. A thin router facilitates, never interprets.

The agents were capable but passive. They responded when asked. They didn't watch.

So I gave each agent a monitoring loop — a periodic scan of its domain, triggered by cron every 30 to 60 minutes, but governed by the agent's own judgment about what deserves my attention.

How It Works

The email agent now scans Gmail for drafts older than two hours. It doesn't alert me about every draft — it judges whether a draft looks like a forgotten send. Addressed to a real person, substantive subject, aging past the point of intent.

When it finds one, I get a Telegram message:

Telegram · Waking System

📧 Unsent draft to [person] about PhD proposal. 3 hours old.
Send or discard?

I see it on my phone while making dinner. One tap. Crisis averted.

Each agent has its own monitoring mandate:

  • The calendar agent watches for meetings in the next seven days that have no preparation task. Three days out, it nudges. One day out, it insists.
  • The task agent notices when my outward queue — emails to send, people to contact, things to share — has been neglected for five or more working days. It says so directly.
  • The network agent flags relationships going stale. Four weeks without contact to a key collaborator triggers a gentle prompt.
  • The note agent notices when I haven't narrated what I'm building in three or more days. The narrations feed a weekly synthesis that drafts blog posts from accumulated observations — including this one.

The Design Principle

The monitoring loops are the most genuinely Hayekian component of the system. No central monitor decides what to check. Each agent watches its own territory using local knowledge no other agent has. The email agent understands what a forgotten draft looks like. The calendar agent understands what an unprepared meeting looks like. The task agent understands what a neglected queue looks like.

They exercise judgment and stay silent when nothing needs attention. The best day is when I hear nothing from them.

What I Learned

Building the monitoring loops taught me something about the difference between tools and colleagues. A tool does what you ask. A colleague notices what you missed. The gap between the two is judgment — the ability to look at a domain and decide, independently, that something needs attention.

My agents are not intelligent in any deep sense. They run heuristics against API responses every thirty minutes. But the system they form — six independent monitors, each scanning a different domain, each applying different judgment criteria, all surfacing through one Telegram channel — creates something that feels less like software and more like a team.

A team that never sleeps, never forgets to check, and never gets annoyed when I don't act on their suggestions.

The unsent draft would have been caught. That's worth the entire build.
